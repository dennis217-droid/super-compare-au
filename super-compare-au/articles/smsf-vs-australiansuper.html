SMSF vs AustralianSuper — Which Is Better for Your Balance?

Updated July 2026  ·  By MySuperChoices

AustralianSuper is Australia's largest super fund with over 3.4 million members and a strong 10-year return track record. An SMSF gives you total control over your investments. But which one actually delivers more value at your balance? The answer depends almost entirely on how much you have — and what you want to do with it.

Fee Comparison at Every Balance Level

AustralianSuper's Balanced option charges an administration fee of $1.50/week plus approximately 0.56% in investment fees and 0.10% in indirect costs — totalling around 0.67–0.70% per year on most balances. We've compared this against a typical SMSF running cost of $4,500 per year:

The Crossover Point

At current AustralianSuper fee rates, an SMSF's fixed cost becomes cheaper once your balance reaches approximately $670,000. Below that, AustralianSuper's percentage-based fee is lower in dollar terms.

Returns Comparison

AustralianSuper's Balanced option has delivered approximately 8.2% per year over the 10 years to June 2026 — placing it consistently among the top-performing balanced funds in Australia. It benefits from its enormous scale to access wholesale investment rates and unlisted infrastructure assets not available to retail investors.

SMSF returns vary enormously depending on how the fund is invested. The ATO's SMSF statistical overview consistently shows that SMSFs in the $200K–$500K range underperform large APRA funds on a net-return basis, largely due to the cost drag. Above $1 million, SMSF returns become more competitive — and for members who implement sophisticated investment strategies, can exceed industry fund returns.

What AustralianSuper Has That an SMSF Doesn't

  • Default insurance cover — life insurance, total and permanent disability (TPD), and income protection are included automatically. Arranging equivalent cover through an SMSF requires a separate policy and is generally more expensive.
  • APRA oversight — the fund is regulated by the Australian Prudential Regulation Authority, providing an additional layer of member protection.
  • No trustee obligations — you're a member, not a trustee. No annual compliance requirements, no audit to arrange, no investment strategy to document.
  • Scale advantages — access to unlisted infrastructure (airports, toll roads, utilities) and private equity at wholesale rates.
  • No minimum balance — works at any balance level, even $1,000.

What an SMSF Has That AustralianSuper Doesn't

  • Direct property ownership — commercial property can be held with borrowing; residential property can be held outright (note: borrowing for new residential property banned from 10 Aug 2026).
  • Business premises strategy — you can buy the premises your business operates from inside your SMSF and pay rent to the fund at arm's length rates.
  • Direct shares and ETFs — you choose exactly which ASX stocks, international ETFs, or listed investment companies to hold.
  • Alternative assets — gold bullion, cryptocurrency, unlisted shares, and private debt can all be held in an SMSF subject to the investment strategy rules.
  • Tax timing control — you decide when to realise capital gains, and can switch to pension phase strategically to reduce tax.
  • Estate planning flexibility — binding death benefit nominations and pension reversionary beneficiary arrangements are far more flexible than most APRA funds allow.

The Verdict

If your balance is under $500,000 and you're not pursuing a specific investment strategy that requires an SMSF, AustralianSuper is almost certainly the better choice. It's cheaper, it requires zero administration on your part, and its long-term performance is genuinely strong.

Above $670,000, the fee advantage shifts to the SMSF — but only if you're running it efficiently and investing it well. If you're going to hold the same diversified index exposure you could get in AustralianSuper, there's little point paying for an SMSF to do it.

The SMSF decision ultimately comes down to strategy, not just fees. If you have a clear reason — commercial property, business premises, direct asset control — the extra cost may well be justified even at lower balances.
