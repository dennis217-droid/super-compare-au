Super Fund Guide

Best Super Fund in Australia 2026 — How to Compare and Choose

Updated July 2026  ·  By MySuperChoices

There is no single "best" super fund in Australia — and anyone who tells you there is is oversimplifying. The right fund depends on your balance, your investment preferences, your appetite for administration, and whether you want to pursue a specific strategy like direct property or business premises. What we can do is give you the data and framework to make the right call for your situation.

The Two Main Choices

Every Australian has a choice between two broad categories of super structure:

APRA-Regulated Funds (Industry and Retail Funds)

These are funds regulated by the Australian Prudential Regulation Authority — including large industry funds like AustralianSuper, Hostplus, Australian Retirement Trust, and HESTA, as well as retail funds run by banks and insurers. They handle all compliance, investing, and administration on your behalf. You're a member, not a trustee. The cost is a percentage of your balance (typically 0.1%–1.1% per year depending on the option and fund).

Self-Managed Super Funds (SMSFs)

You and up to five other members act as trustees and make all investment decisions. You're responsible for all compliance — audit, tax return, investment strategy, and ATO reporting. Costs are largely fixed ($3,500–$6,000/year for most funds), making SMSFs cost-effective only at higher balances (generally $350,000–$500,000+).

What to Look for When Comparing Super Funds

1. Fees (The Single Biggest Controllable Factor)

Over 30 years, a 0.5% difference in fees can cost hundreds of thousands of dollars in foregone compound growth. Always compare the total annual fee — including administration fees, investment fees, and indirect cost ratios — not just the headline number. Some funds advertise a low admin fee but charge high investment fees that swamp the saving.

2. Net Returns After Fees

Gross returns are meaningless without considering fees. Compare the net benefit ratio — what members actually receive after all costs are deducted. SuperRatings and Chant West publish independently calculated net benefit comparisons annually.

3. Insurance Cover

All APRA funds provide default life insurance, total and permanent disability (TPD) cover, and often income protection. The default terms, premiums, and coverage amounts vary significantly. If you have dependants or a mortgage, review the default cover carefully — and compare it to what you'd need to arrange independently through an SMSF.

4. Investment Options

Most large industry funds offer a range of options from cash and fixed income through to high-growth equity portfolios. Some offer direct investment platforms (e.g. AustralianSuper's Member Direct, Hostplus's Choiceplus) that allow members to buy direct shares and ETFs within the fund — bridging some of the gap with an SMSF.

5. Fund Size and Stability

Larger funds benefit from scale — lower wholesale investment costs, better access to unlisted assets, and more resources for member services. That said, very large funds can face liquidity constraints during market stress. The top 10 Australian industry funds are all well-capitalised and APRA-regulated.

The SMSF Option for High-Balance Members

For members with balances above $500,000 — particularly those pursuing commercial property, business premises, or specific asset strategies — an SMSF can outperform industry funds on both cost and flexibility. The fixed annual running cost (~$4,500/year for a typical fund) becomes a smaller percentage at higher balances, eventually making it cheaper than the percentage-based fees charged by even the most competitive APRA funds.

However, the compliance obligation is real. SMSF trustees spend an average of several hours per month on fund administration. If you're not prepared for that responsibility, even a cost-competitive SMSF may not be the right choice.
